The Kosher Cheese Primer

An introduction for cheesemakers on producing kosher products

First, why kosher?

What is kosher and why should you care about it?

Colloquially, kosher refers to any food that's suitable to eat under the Jewish dietary laws. The origins are biblical, but many people today recognize the kosher system as humanity's earliest attempt to implement a more humane and self-aware approach to eating.

Kosher products are now valued across the religious and dietary spectrum:

Jewish consumers

Of the 6 million Jews in the U.S., about 15% "keep kosher" (i.e. observe the dietary laws). These folks buy exclusively kosher-certified products, which is great news for your dairy business, since the current kosher cheese selection is severely limited, and of generally low quality.

Non-Jewish consumers

Nearly 80% of all kosher food sales are outside the traditional Jewish market. Many people use kosher certifications to navigate their own dietary restrictions, including:

  • Allergen-sensitive individuals (up to 15 million Americans)
  • Vegetarians (7.3 million) and the vegetarian-inclined (22.8 million)
  • Muslims (2.6 million)
  • Seventh-day Adventists (1.2 million in North America)
So, what makes cheese kosher?

Kosher cheese: nearly identical to non-kosher

Apart from some basic sanitization and supervision procedures, producing kosher cheese is nearly identical to how you're already producing cheese. Here's what you need to make your cheeses kosher:

Kosher Ingredients

Fortunately, all milks that are suitable for cheesemaking are kosher, e.g. cow, goat, sheep. Your starter cultures, flavorings, and rennet will need to be kosher certified. Most commercially available cultures and vegetable or microbial rennets are already certified.

Kosher dairy products may not contain any meat-based ingredients, hence the need for non-animal rennet.

See the Resources section below for a listing of the many kosher cultures and coagulants available.

Kosher Sanitization

Any equipment, utensils, or work surfaces that have been previously used for non-kosher production may require a kosher sanitization, or kashering, process after a 24-hour period of non-use.

Alternatively, work surfaces may be covered during kosher production, and non-kosher utensils may be swapped out with new, unused utensils.

Kosher Supervision

A kosher supervisor, or mashgiach, will assist you with identifying appropriate kosher ingredients, identifying which equipment requires sanitization, performing any necessary sanitization, and developing a plan for tracking the cheeses during aging (if appropriate).

The supervisor must also be involved in adding the coagulant to the milk — under your culinary direction, of course.
